## Post 1: The Real Cost of Manual AP Processing

**Target Audience:** Accounts Payable (AP) teams, Controllers, Finance Managers

**Why this topic matters:** AP processing is a daily pain point. Most finance teams spend 40-60% of their time on manual data entry and reconciliation. This post speaks directly to the frustration and offers a practical reframe.

---

**Post:**

Every morning, Sarah opens 47 invoices, 3 spreadsheets, and 2 browser tabs to do what she did yesterday. Again.

If you're in AP, you know Sarah.

Here's what nobody tells you about manual AP processing:

1. **It's not just slow — it's risky.** Duplicate payments, missed discounts, and data entry errors cost companies an average of 1-2% of annual revenue.

2. **It masks real problems.** When your team is busy entering data, they don't have time to ask "why did we spend 3x on vendor X this month?"

3. **It burns your best people.** Your sharpest finance operators get bored. They leave. And then you're training someone new to do the same manual work.

What if we flipped the script?

Instead of asking "how do we process invoices faster," ask "what would our AP team do if they had 20 hours back every week?"

The answer is usually: negotiate better terms, catch anomalies early, improve vendor relationships.

That's not just AP work. That's strategic finance work.

Stop treating AP as a processing center. Start treating it as a intelligence center.

## Post 2: The Metric That Actually Predicts SaaS Burn

**Target Audience:** CFOs, FP&A professionals, Finance Operators

**Why this topic matters:** Most finance teams track burn multiple the wrong way. Leading indicators are underused in SaaS finance. This post positions the reader as someone who thinks ahead.

---

**Post:**

Every CFO I know tracks burn multiple.

But most are looking in the rearview mirror.

Here's the problem with standard burn multiple:
- It's lagging (looks at past 12 months)
- It averages out your best and worst months
- It tells you where you've been, not where you're headed

Try this instead:

**Leading Burn Ratio = (Current Month Net Burn) / (New ARR Signed This Month)**

Why this works:
- It measures efficiency in real-time
- It forces you to look at your sales engine, not just your spend
- A rising ratio tells you to tighten spend BEFORE cash becomes an issue

Try benchmarking your last 3 months against this. If your Leading Burn Ratio is trending up, you have a signal — not a surprise — 60 days before your board asks.

Your FP&A team doesn't need more reports. They need better signals.

## Post 3: The Follow-Up That Closes

**Target Audience:** Finance Ops teams, RevOps, Sales Finance, Growth teams

**Why this topic matters:** The gap between signed contract and paid invoice is where cash flow dies. This post addresses a practical, cross-functional pain point that finance ops teams feel daily.

---

**Post:**

The deal is signed. High fives all around.

Then the invoice sits unpaid for 47 days.

Here's what finance teams know that sales teams don't:

**The collection conversation is just as important as the closing conversation.**

Best practice I've seen from top finance ops teams:

1. **Send the invoice within 2 hours of signing.** Not 2 days. Momentum matters. Every hour of delay costs you 0.5 days in DSO.

2. **Include a payment reminder schedule in the welcome email.** "We'll send a reminder on day 15, day 30, and day 45 — unless you want a different cadence." Sets expectations. Reduces friction.

3. **Call before you email.** A 2-minute phone call on day 20 resolves what 6 email threads can't. Tone is everything.

4. **Offer incentives for early payment.** A 2% discount for payment within 10 days beats chasing invoices for 60 days. Math works every time.

The best finance teams don't just track cash. They accelerate it.

And sometimes the biggest improvement isn't a new tool. It's a new conversation.
